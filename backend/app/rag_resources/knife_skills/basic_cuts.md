# Technique: Basic Cuts

## Category
knife_skills

## What It Is
Basic cuts are the standard ways professional and home cooks break food down into consistent pieces. The four most useful for beginners are: chop (rough, uneven pieces), dice (uniform cubes in small/medium/large sizes), mince (very fine, almost paste-like pieces), and julienne (long, thin matchstick strips). Each cut is suited to specific cooking situations.

## Why It Matters
Cut size directly controls how fast food cooks and how it feels in your mouth. If you dice an onion unevenly — some pieces large, some tiny — the small pieces burn while the large ones are still raw. Consistency isn't about being precious; it's about everything finishing at the same time and tasting right. Knowing which cut to use also helps you understand recipes faster: "mince the garlic" tells you it should dissolve into the dish, not stay in detectable chunks.

## How To Do It
**For a dice:**
1. Trim the ends off your vegetable and peel if needed.
2. Cut it into flat planks of even thickness (for a medium dice, about ½ inch thick).
3. Stack or line up the planks and cut them into strips of the same width.
4. Turn the strips 90 degrees and cut across them to form cubes.

**For a mince:**
1. Complete the dice steps above until you have small pieces.
2. Place your non-dominant hand flat on top of the knife tip to anchor it.
3. Rock the heel of the knife up and down, pivoting around the tip, sweeping back and forth across the pile until pieces are very fine.

**For a julienne:**
1. Cut your vegetable into sections about 2–3 inches long.
2. Slice each section into thin planks, about ⅛ inch thick.
3. Stack the planks and cut them lengthwise into thin matchstick strips of the same width.

**For a rough chop:**
1. Cut the food into manageable chunks first.
2. Cut across them quickly without worrying about uniformity — aim for pieces that are roughly similar in size but not precise.

## What To Watch For
- **Dice:** When you look at your pile of cut vegetables, the pieces should look like a uniform field — very few outliers that are dramatically larger or smaller.
- **Mince:** Properly minced garlic almost disappears into a dish. If you can still see distinct chunks, keep going.
- **Julienne:** The strips should be thin enough to bend slightly without snapping. Thicker than a matchstick means they won't cook evenly with the rest of the dish.
- **Sound:** A confident, consistent chopping rhythm means your cuts are even. Erratic sounds usually mean uneven pieces.
- **Touch:** When you push a pile of diced vegetables around, they should all feel and look about the same size — no big stragglers.

## Common Mistakes
- **Skipping the flat side:** Round vegetables roll and slip. Always cut a flat side first before attempting any other cut — this is the most important safety step in all knife work.
- **Inconsistent plank thickness:** The dice only works if your initial planks are even. Rushing through this step creates a chaotic final cut that cooks unevenly.
- **Mincing with the whole blade moving:** Many beginners lift the entire knife off the board. Keep the tip anchored and only rock the heel — you get more control and speed.
- **Julienne strips too thick:** Beginners tend to cut julienne too conservatively (too thick) because thin cuts feel risky. Trust the bear claw grip, go thinner, and you'll get better results.

## What Success Looks Like
You can dice half an onion into roughly ½-inch cubes in under two minutes, with most pieces looking similar in size. You can mince two garlic cloves until no large chunks remain. Your julienned carrots or zucchini look like actual matchsticks, not small planks.

## Practice Mission
Your mission: dice one medium onion into a small dice (about ¼-inch cubes). Time: 15 minutes. Focus only on: making your planks, strips, and final cuts all the same thickness before you speed up.
