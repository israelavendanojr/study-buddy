# Technique: Bear Claw Grip

## Category
knife_skills

## What It Is
The bear claw is the correct hand position for holding food while you cut it. You curl your fingers inward so your knuckles face the blade, your fingertips are tucked safely behind them, and the flat side of the blade rests against your knuckles as a guide. Your thumb stays tucked behind your fingers, never sticking out to the side.

## Why It Matters
Without a safe grip, your fingertips are directly in the blade's path — and knife injuries in the kitchen almost always happen because someone was holding food with flat, extended fingers. The bear claw does two things at once: it protects your fingers from cuts, and it gives you a stable, controlled surface to guide the knife. The knuckle acts as a physical fence that prevents the blade from traveling too far forward. This means you cut more accurately, more quickly, and with far less tension in your hands.

## How To Do It
1. Place your food flat-side-down on the cutting board so it doesn't rock.
2. Rest your non-dominant hand on top of the food.
3. Curl all four fingers inward so your fingertips point straight down into the food.
4. Tuck your thumb behind your index finger — it should not be visible from the front.
5. Let your knuckles face the blade, forming a vertical wall.
6. Rest the flat side of the knife blade lightly against your middle knuckle.
7. Begin cutting, moving the knife forward and down in a rocking motion.
8. After each cut, walk your fingers backward across the food to advance your next slice — keeping the claw shape the entire time.

## What To Watch For
- **Correct:** Your knuckles are touching the blade's flat side throughout the cut — you'll feel light contact, like a guide rail.
- **Correct:** Your fingertips are curled completely under and never visible above the knuckles from the blade's perspective.
- **Wrong:** If your thumb is sticking out to the side, stop and tuck it back — this is the most common danger point.
- **Wrong:** If the blade is drifting away from your knuckles, you're not guiding the cut — reposition and keep contact.
- **Correct:** Your hand should feel stable and slightly tense, like you're gripping a tennis ball.

## Common Mistakes
- **Flat fingers:** The most dangerous habit. Beginners instinctively lay their hand flat because it feels "secure," but it puts fingertips directly in the blade's path. Consciously curl every time.
- **Thumb sticking out:** The thumb often escapes the claw without you noticing. Check your thumb position every few cuts until it becomes automatic.
- **Losing blade contact:** When the knife drifts away from the knuckle guide, cuts become uneven and control disappears. Keep gentle contact between blade and knuckle on every stroke.
- **Rushing the walk-back:** Beginners sometimes slide their hand backward without re-establishing the claw. Pause, reset the grip, then move your hand — don't let speed override safety.

## What Success Looks Like
You can cut a carrot, onion, or potato into even slices without hesitating or repositioning your hand between cuts. Your knife stays controlled and your fingers feel genuinely safe — not just "probably fine." Over time, the claw becomes so automatic you stop thinking about it.

## Practice Mission
Your mission: slice one whole carrot into rounds of roughly equal thickness using only the bear claw grip. Time: 10 minutes. Focus only on: keeping your fingertips curled under and your knuckle touching the blade on every single cut.
