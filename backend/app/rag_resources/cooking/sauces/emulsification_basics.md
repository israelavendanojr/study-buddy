# Technique: Emulsification Basics

## Category
sauces

## What It Is
Emulsification is the process of combining two liquids that don't naturally mix — usually fat (oil or butter) and water (stock, juice, vinegar) — into a stable, creamy, uniform mixture. The result is a sauce that is smooth, thick, and cohesive rather than greasy and separated. Mayonnaise, hollandaise, vinaigrette, and a properly mounted butter sauce are all emulsions.

## Why It Matters
Oil and water don't want to mix — left alone, fat rises and separates. An emulsion holds them together through two mechanisms: an emulsifier (like egg yolk lecithin, mustard, or even the proteins in butter) that coats fat droplets and keeps them suspended, and physical agitation (whisking, blending) that breaks fat into tiny droplets and distributes them. Without understanding this, sauces break: you get oily puddles floating on watery liquid. Understanding it means you know how to prevent breaks before they happen — and how to rescue a sauce when they do.

## How To Do It
**Making a basic vinaigrette (temporary emulsion):**
1. Combine vinegar, a pinch of salt, and a small amount of mustard in a bowl or jar. The mustard is your emulsifier.
2. Whisk or shake vigorously while slowly adding oil in a thin, steady stream.
3. The mixture will turn creamy and opaque as the emulsion forms. Use immediately or shake well before using, as vinaigrette breaks over time.

**Mounting butter into a pan sauce (temporary emulsion):**
1. Reduce your sauce to a syrupy consistency.
2. Remove from heat or keep on very low heat — too much heat breaks a butter emulsion.
3. Add cold butter, one small cube at a time, swirling the pan constantly as each piece melts.
4. The sauce will become glossy and slightly thickened as the emulsion builds.

**Making mayonnaise (stable emulsion):**
1. Place an egg yolk in a bowl with a small amount of mustard and a few drops of lemon juice.
2. Whisk vigorously while adding neutral oil in an extremely slow, thin stream — drop by drop for the first tablespoon.
3. As the emulsion builds, you can add oil faster. The mixture will thicken dramatically.
4. Season with salt and more lemon juice to taste.

**To rescue a broken sauce:**
1. Pour off the broken sauce into a separate bowl.
2. Add a new emulsifier base to the original vessel (a fresh egg yolk, a teaspoon of mustard, or just a tablespoon of reduced sauce).
3. Slowly whisk or drizzle the broken sauce back in, treating it like the oil in a fresh emulsion.

## What To Watch For
- **Forming correctly:** A vinaigrette or mayonnaise will visibly thicken, turn opaque, and become creamy as the emulsion builds. A butter sauce will turn glossy and coat the back of a spoon.
- **About to break:** The sauce will look slightly greasy around the edges, or pools of fat will appear on the surface. This is your warning — reduce heat immediately and add a small amount of cold liquid while whisking.
- **Broken:** Clear separation of fat and water, with oily puddles floating above thin liquid. Fully broken — but fixable if caught quickly.
- **Temperature warning:** Butter sauces break above approximately 160°F. If the pan is too hot, the butter fat melts out of the emulsion entirely. Keep the heat low.
- **Texture of mayonnaise:** Properly emulsified mayo should hold a peak when you lift the whisk — it has structure, not just thickness.

## Common Mistakes
- **Adding oil too fast:** This is the most common cause of broken vinaigrette and mayonnaise. The emulsifier can only coat a small amount of fat at a time — overwhelm it with too much oil at once and the emulsion never forms. Go slowly at the start.
- **Overheating butter sauces:** Heat breaks the bond between fat and water in a butter emulsion. Keep the heat on low or pull the pan entirely off the heat while adding butter.
- **Skipping the emulsifier:** You can emulsify without mustard or egg yolk, but the result is fragile and breaks quickly. Even a small amount of mustard in a vinaigrette dramatically extends how long it stays emulsified.
- **Giving up on a broken sauce:** Most broken sauces can be rescued. People throw them out unnecessarily. Try the rescue technique before starting over.

## What Success Looks Like
You can make a simple vinaigrette that holds together for at least several minutes after whisking. You can mount butter into a pan sauce until it turns glossy and coats a spoon. When a sauce breaks, you recognize what happened and know how to fix it rather than throwing it out and starting over.
