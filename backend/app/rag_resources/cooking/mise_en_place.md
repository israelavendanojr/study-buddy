# Technique: Mise en Place

## Category
general

## What It Is
Mise en place is a French culinary term meaning "everything in its place." It's the practice of preparing, measuring, and organizing every ingredient and tool you'll need before you start cooking — so that when the heat is on, you're simply executing, not scrambling. It means diced onion in a small bowl, garlic already minced, herbs measured, spices pre-portioned, and your pan already on the stove before you light the first burner.

## Why It Matters
Cooking involves real-time decisions, precise timing, and multiple things happening simultaneously. If you're trying to mince garlic while your onions are burning, or measuring spices while a sauce reduces too fast, something always suffers. Mise en place removes these collisions — it separates the thinking and prep work from the cooking work so you can do each well. Professional kitchens operate this way because speed and consistency depend on it. Home cooks who adopt this practice report that cooking becomes calmer, more enjoyable, and more consistently successful — because they're never behind.

## How To Do It
1. Read the entire recipe before touching anything. Understand what comes first, what takes the longest, and what has to happen simultaneously.
2. Set out every ingredient on your counter. If something is in the pantry or fridge, take it out now — including butter to soften, proteins to come to room temperature, and anything frozen.
3. Wash, peel, and prepare all vegetables before you begin cutting.
4. Cut everything according to the recipe — dice the onion, mince the garlic, slice the meat. Place each prepared ingredient in its own small bowl or section of a cutting board.
5. Measure all dry spices and seasonings into small bowls or the lids of their containers.
6. Set out all tools: the right pans, spoons, ladles, tongs, thermometer.
7. Pre-heat the oven if required.
8. Only then turn on the stove and begin cooking.

## What To Watch For
- **You're ready when:** You can look at your counter and see every ingredient the recipe calls for, prepped and in its own container. Nothing is still whole that needs to be cut. Nothing is still in the pantry that needs to be out.
- **You're not ready when:** You're still chopping vegetables after you've turned on the burner. You're hunting for the cumin while something is on heat. You realize mid-cook that you're out of an ingredient.
- **A well mise-en-placed cook:** Moves smoothly from step to step, never stops to think "what's next" or "where is the X," and has bandwidth to pay attention to the food — its color, smell, and texture — rather than logistics.
- **The wrong version:** Counter covered in original packaging, nothing measured, still cutting while something's in the pan — this is the version that produces inconsistent results and stressful cooking.

## Common Mistakes
- **Prepping "as you go" for timed recipes:** This works for slow braises where you have 20 minutes while something simmers. It doesn't work for stir-fries, pan sauces, or anything where multiple elements need attention simultaneously. Know which recipes demand complete mise en place.
- **Skipping the recipe read-through:** Starting to prep without reading the full recipe is how you discover (too late) that the dough needs to chill for two hours, or that you've been mincing when you needed slices. Read it all, then prep.
- **Using large containers for everything:** Using a large mixing bowl to hold all your prepped vegetables seems efficient until you dump the wrong thing at the wrong time. Small individual containers — ramekins, cups, even the lids of spice jars — keep each element separate and prevent errors.
- **Not accounting for what's already mise en placed by the recipe:** Some steps in recipes double as prep for later steps. Know which steps are sequential and which are simultaneous so you're not over-prepping unnecessarily.

## What Success Looks Like
You read a recipe, prep every ingredient before lighting the stove, and cook the dish from start to finish without stopping, searching, or scrambling. You have mental bandwidth to notice what the food looks, smells, and sounds like at each stage. The dish comes out tasting right because you were paying attention, not managing chaos.

## Practice Mission
Your mission: choose any recipe you've made before and practice full mise en place — prep every single ingredient, measured and ready, before you turn on the stove. Time: 30 minutes of prep, then cook. Focus only on: identifying the moment when you're truly ready to begin, with nothing left to prepare.
