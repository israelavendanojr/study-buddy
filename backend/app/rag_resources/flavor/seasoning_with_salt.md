# Technique: Seasoning with Salt

## Category
flavor

## What It Is
Seasoning with salt means adding salt deliberately at multiple stages of cooking — not just at the end — and tasting as you go to develop and adjust flavor progressively. It's not about making food taste salty; it's about using salt to make other flavors more fully present and to bring a dish into balance.

## Why It Matters
Salt does something chemically interesting: it suppresses bitterness, enhances sweetness, and amplifies savory flavors — all at once. Under-salted food doesn't just taste bland; it tastes flat in a way that more of everything else can't fix. Over-salted food is harder to fix (you can dilute a dish, but it's a pain). The skill is in adding salt in layers — a little to the pasta water, a pinch while cooking vegetables, a final adjustment at the end — so the flavor develops as the dish develops, rather than dumping it all in at the end and hoping. Salt added during cooking integrates; salt added at the table sits on top.

## How To Do It
1. Season your pasta or vegetable water generously before cooking — it should taste pleasantly salty, like mild ocean water. This is the only time food has to absorb salt at volume.
2. When cooking vegetables or proteins in a pan, add a pinch of salt when they first go in. This draws out a small amount of moisture and begins building flavor from the start.
3. Taste the food partway through cooking — not just at the end. Ask: does this taste flat? Flat = under-seasoned.
4. Add salt in small increments: a pinch at a time (about ⅛ teaspoon of kosher salt). Stir or toss to distribute.
5. Wait 30 seconds, then taste again. Salt takes a moment to integrate — tasting immediately after adding it gives you an incomplete picture.
6. Before serving, taste one final time. This is your last chance to adjust.
7. When adjusting at the end, add salt and a tiny squeeze of lemon or splash of vinegar together — they work synergistically to lift flat flavor.

## What To Watch For
- **Under-salted:** The food tastes muted, two-dimensional, or "missing something" even though the other flavors are present. Everything tastes vaguely the same.
- **Properly seasoned:** Individual flavors pop — the tomato tastes more tomato-y, the garlic comes through, the sweetness of the onion registers. The dish has dimension.
- **Over-salted:** Your lips or the tip of your tongue start to tingle unpleasantly. The saltiness overwhelms any other flavor you're looking for.
- **Watch out with high-sodium ingredients:** Soy sauce, fish sauce, anchovies, canned stock, and parmesan all contribute significant salt. Taste before adding more salt to any dish that includes these.

## Common Mistakes
- **Only salting at the end:** Salt sprinkled on finished food tastes sharp and sits on the surface. Salt added during cooking mellows and integrates. You need both — during cooking and a final taste — but not only at the end.
- **Being afraid of salt:** Many beginners chronically under-season because they fear over-salting. Under-seasoned food is the more common problem in home kitchens, by a wide margin. Trust the process of small increments and tasting.
- **Not tasting as you cook:** You can't season what you haven't tasted. Tasting is not optional — it's the entire skill.
- **Using table salt interchangeably with kosher salt:** Table salt is much finer and roughly twice as dense as kosher salt by volume. A teaspoon of table salt is significantly saltier than a teaspoon of kosher salt. If a recipe is calibrated for kosher salt and you use table salt at the same amount, you'll over-season. Know what you're using.

## What Success Looks Like
You can taste a dish partway through cooking, identify that it's under-seasoned, add a small amount of salt, taste again 30 seconds later, and notice a real improvement. Your finished food reliably has dimension and presence — people can taste what the dish is supposed to taste like, not just a flat version of it.

## Practice Mission
Your mission: make a simple sautéed vegetable (zucchini, mushrooms, or green beans) and season it in three stages — when it goes in the pan, partway through cooking, and just before plating. Taste at each stage. Time: 15 minutes. Focus only on: noticing what changes between each tasting.
